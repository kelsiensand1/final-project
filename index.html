Home health aides neglected through and through

By Kelsie Sandoval
Published May. 20, 2020 8:00 PM

The coronavirus has shed light on how healthcare workers have treated patients despite the risks of getting infected. Daily cheers, news coverage and community efforts have all praised how healthcare workers have risen to the challenge in saving patients during the coronavirus pandemic. Yet, home health aides rarely get lauded, even though they continue to go to work every day and risk infecting themselves, their clients and their family members. 2018 midterms.

Not only are home health aides continuing to go to work, they are not getting support from their employers. In addition to these dangerous conditions, home health aides make on average $26,240 a year.

“You think since we’re in healthcare, they’d provide us with ppe,” said Kawehkadawije Dailey. A home health aide in Newark, New York, Dailey feels frustrated that her employer, the Center for Disability Rights, has not provided face masks or gloves. But she’s still continuing to go to work because she loves her clients.

Public home health agencies are funded through Medicaid. And while other states have paid home health aides an extra $2 an hour through hazard pay, New York has not designated any hazard pay to home health aides.

Home health aides have a history of being neglected, according to Allison Cook who is policy mangers at PHI, a nonprofit dedicated to doing research on behalf of direct care workers. “If anything, this is highlighting the existing challenges within the healthcare system,” she said.

Heather Chadwich is another home health aid continuing to go work. She is taking extra precautions because her husband is going through cancer treatment and is another individual vulnerable to the disease. “I’m still going to work because I love the family,” she said.
